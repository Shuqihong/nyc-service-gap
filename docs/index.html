NYC 311 Urban Inequality Analysis

Why Do Some New Yorkers
Wait Longer for City Services?

The Hidden Story Behind 3 Million 311 Calls

New York City · Full Year 2024 · 3,187,149 Closed Complaints · 177 Neighborhoods

March 7, 2024. Two New Yorkers wake up without heat. Both call 311, both get routed to the NYC Department of Housing Preservation and Development (HPD), and both describe the problem in nearly the same words.

On the surface, their situations look identical. But one lives in Cambria Heights, Queens, where the median household income is $147,000. The other lives in Soundview, the South Bronx, where it’s $38,000.

The Queens resident had heat by lunchtime. The Bronx resident waited 17 days.

What is NYC 311?

311 is New York City’s non-emergency government services hotline. Residents call, text, or go online to report problems like broken street lights, noise complaints, lack of heat, or unsanitary conditions. Each request is logged, routed to the responsible agency, and tracked until resolution. The city publishes every request as open data.

These are two real complaints, pulled from the city’s own records. Is the city treating rich and poor neighborhoods differently, or is something else going on?

To find out, I worked through every closed 311 service request filed in New York City in 2024, more than three million complaints across 177 neighborhoods. The data points to something more specific than simple discrimination.

The Wait Gap

Sorting New York’s 177 neighborhoods by median household income and dividing them into four equal-sized quartiles makes the pattern clear. The poorest quartile (Q1) waits a median of 10.8 hours for a 311 complaint to close. The wealthiest (Q4) waits 3.9 hours, almost three times faster. Q2 and Q3 sit between them at roughly seven hours each. Lower-income neighborhoods do tend to wait longer, but the relationship is not a smooth gradient.

The staircase emerges at the group level. At the level of individual complaints, though, the relationship between neighborhood income and resolution time is remarkably weak. Across all 3.2 million records, the Pearson correlation is r = 0.046, very close to zero. Knowing the income of a complaint’s neighborhood tells us almost nothing about how long that one complaint will take to close. The gap is real, but income, on its own, does not explain it.

What is Pearson correlation (r)?

Pearson correlation r measures linear association between two variables and ranges from −1 to +1. The sign gives direction (positive or negative), and the absolute value gives strength. Values near 0 mean almost no linear relationship.

What Drives the Wait

To see which variables actually drive the wait, I fit a sequence of nested regression models on five candidate predictors and watch how much each one adds to the fit. The five are median household income (log-transformed), the total number of 311 complaints filed in each neighborhood, complaint type, filing channel, and city agency. Each is added in turn, on top of those already in the model.

What is nested regression?

Nested regression fits a sequence of models where each step adds a new predictor block on top of the previous one. This shows how much model fit improves as more information is introduced.

What is R²?

R² ranges from 0 to 1 and measures the share of outcome variation explained by the model. Multiply by 100 to read it as a percentage (for example, R² = 0.778 means 77.8% explained variation). Higher R² means better in-sample fit.

Income quartile and neighborhood complaint volume, on their own, account for only 0.2% of the variation in resolution time. Adding complaint type lifts R² to 77.8%. Filing channel contributes essentially nothing on top of that, and city agency adds another 1.2 percentage points, bringing the model to 78.9%. Once we know what kind of problem a complaint is about, almost nothing else carries new information about how long it will take to resolve.

The regression identifies complaint type as the driver. Let’s quantify how much of the actual Q1–Q4 wait gap is attributable to that difference in mix. Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition builds a counterfactual Q1 that keeps its own within-type response speeds but takes Q4’s complaint mix, then computes what Q1’s expected wait would be in that scenario.

What is Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition?

A statistical technique originally developed in labor economics to decompose a gap between two groups into two parts: the portion explained by differences in observable characteristics (such as complaint type mix), and a residual that remains after controlling for those differences. Here it operates on the raw ~158 complaint-type strings (HEAT/HOT WATER, PLUMBING, Noise — Residential, etc.), not on broader category buckets.

Where does 97.8% come from?

The type-weighted median wait is 12.62 h for Q1 and 1.50 h for Q4, a gap of 11.12 h. If Q1 had Q4’s complaint mix but kept its own response speeds, Q1’s expected wait drops to 1.74 h, leaving only a 0.24 h residual. The explained share is (11.12 − 0.24) / 11.12 = 97.8%.

Under that counterfactual, Q1’s expected wait falls to 1.7 hours, almost indistinguishable from Q4’s actual 1.5. Complaint mix alone accounts for 97.8% of the gap between the two quartiles. Whatever within-type speed difference remains can explain at most the other 2.2%.

The Mix Behind the Gap

Let’s look more closely at what is happening inside the complaint mix. The raw data contains roughly two hundred distinct complaint strings, too many to reason about directly. I group them into four categories built around where the broken asset sits and who has to fix it. They are Interior Housing, Public Infrastructure, Quality of Life, and Other. Hover over any category to see the complaints it covers.

The four categories occupy strikingly different speeds. Quality of Life closes in about an hour, Other in two to three, Public Infrastructure in roughly two to three days, and Interior Housing in nearly five. Within each category, the median resolution time barely moves across income quartiles. What changes from Q1 to Q4 is not how fast each kind of complaint is handled, but how often each kind is filed in the first place.

The stacked composition chart traces that shift across the income ladder. The poorer the neighborhood, the larger the share of its 311 stream taken up by slow-resolving complaints. Interior Housing accounts for 34.6% of Q1’s complaints, 22.8% in Q2, 17.6% in Q3, and only 12.1% in Q4. Q4’s stream is dominated instead by Quality of Life and Other complaints, which together make up roughly 76% of its total and almost all of which close within a day.

Looking at the data over time

Tracked month by month, Q2, Q3, and Q4 stay within a relatively narrow band throughout the year, with only a slight rise in winter. Q4, the highest-income quartile, consistently has the shortest resolution time in almost every month. Q1 is the exception. Its monthly median swings dramatically, climbing well above the rest in winter, especially in December and January, and dropping substantially through late spring and summer.

The above chart explains why. HEAT/HOT WATER complaints rise sharply in winter, and the rise is far steeper in lower-income neighborhoods. In December, Q1 files 88.2 heating complaints per 10,000 residents, more than four times Q4’s rate of 21.6. By summer, the rate falls to near zero in every quartile. Heating problems concentrate in poorer neighborhoods during the coldest months, then enter 311 as the same slow Interior Housing complaints already visible in the cross-sectional view.

Why, then, do Q1 neighborhoods file so many more of these complaints to begin with, especially HEAT/HOT WATER in winter?

Upstream of 311

311 is the city’s intake system for non-emergency requests and complaints. It records issues reported by residents and routes them to the city department responsible for each one. The diagram below traces two different paths a complaint can take through the system, depending on what kind of problem it describes.

For housing problems, 311 is not the first step but an escalation mechanism. Tenants are expected to contact their landlord first, and they file a 311 complaint only after the landlord has delayed, refused, or failed to act. The case is then routed to HPD, which may inspect the building, issue violations, and apply legal or administrative pressure on the landlord. 311 does not repair anything itself; what it does is turn an ignored private request into a documented public matter that the city can enforce.

For public-space problems, there is no private owner to escalate to, so residents report directly to 311. The system functions here as a dispatcher, routing each case automatically to the city agency responsible for handling it.

That asymmetry is what makes the housing pathway so unevenly distributed across neighborhoods. In low-rent buildings, owners often calculate that the cost of replacing a failing boiler or repairing chronic plumbing leaks exceeds the marginal rent they can extract from the unit, and they defer the work, sometimes indefinitely. Tenants with little bargaining power have no way to force the repair privately, and when the landlord stops answering, 311 becomes the only lever they have left. HEAT/HOT WATER, plumbing, and pest complaints pile up in the dataset as a result. Matthew Desmond’s fieldwork in “Evicted” (2016) documents the underlying dynamic at length, and NYU’s Furman Center has shown again and again that the most serious housing deficiencies cluster in the city’s poorest ZIP codes.

Higher-income neighborhoods rarely generate the same volume of housing tickets, but not because their buildings never break. When a leak appears in an owner-occupied apartment, the owner simply calls a plumber. When it appears in a well-maintained rental, a responsive landlord fixes it quickly to protect the rent roll and the building’s value. The problem is resolved privately, before 311 is ever dialed, and it never enters the dataset. Public-space complaints from wealthier areas still flow in as usual; the housing branch simply carries lighter traffic. What looks like a higher complaint volume in poorer neighborhoods is, at root, a record of failed private maintenance, of tenants left with no option but to escalate to the city.

Conclusion

Q1 neighborhoods wait roughly three times longer than Q4 for a 311 complaint to close, but the gap is not a story of differential treatment. The Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition attributes 97.8% of the difference to what residents report rather than to how fast each kind of complaint is handled. Most of what poorer neighborhoods report is interior housing problems, inherently slow to resolve, that concentrate further in winter as heating systems fail in older rental stock.

The root cause sits upstream of 311. For public-space problems the system dispatches directly to a city agency. For housing, it activates only after a tenant’s private request to a landlord has been ignored. Wealthier neighborhoods solve the same problems privately and never enter the dataset; poorer ones depend on 311 as their last lever. The equity gap in resolution time is, at root, a housing infrastructure crisis made visible through the city’s own records.
