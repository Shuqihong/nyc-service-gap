NYC 311 Urban Inequality Analysis

Why Do Some New Yorkers
Wait Longer for City Services?

The Hidden Story Behind 3 Million 311 Calls

New York City · Full Year 2024 · 3,187,149 Closed Complaints · 177 Neighborhoods

March 7, 2024. Two New Yorkers wake up to the same emergency: no heat. Both call 311, both get routed to HPD, and both describe the problem in nearly the same words.

On the surface, their situations look identical. But one lives in Cambria Heights, Queens, where the median household income is $147,000. The other lives in Soundview, the South Bronx, where it’s $38,000.

The Queens resident had heat by lunchtime. The Bronx resident waited 17 days.

What is NYC 311?

311 is New York City’s non-emergency government services hotline. Residents call, text, or go online to report problems like broken street lights, noise complaints, lack of heat, or unsanitary conditions. Each request is logged, routed to the responsible agency, and tracked until resolution. The city publishes every request as open data.

These are two real complaints pulled from the city’s own records. Is the city treating rich and poor neighborhoods differently, or is something else going on?

To find out, we analyzed every closed 311 service request filed in New York City in 2024, over 3 million complaints across 177 neighborhoods. The data reveals something more specific than simple discrimination.

The Gap

New York’s 177 neighborhoods, grouped by income into four quartiles, show a clear gap. The poorest quarter (Q1) waits a median of 10.8 hours for a 311 complaint to close. The wealthiest quarter (Q4) waits 3.9 hours. Nearly three times as long. Q2 and Q3 fall in between at around 7 hours, forming a staircase from the poorest to the wealthiest neighborhoods.

The staircase is real. But across all 3.2 million complaints, the correlation between neighborhood income and resolution time is just r = 0.046, indicating a very weak relationship. Income alone barely predicts how long any given complaint takes to resolve, so something else is driving the pattern.

What is Pearson correlation (r)?

Pearson correlation r measures linear association between two variables and ranges from −1 to +1. The sign gives direction (positive or negative), and the absolute value gives strength. Values near 0 mean almost no linear relationship.

Statistical Analysis

Before accepting “income causes longer waits,” we have to test it. Across 3.2 million complaints we measured how strongly each candidate variable moves with resolution time, and then asked which of those effects survive once we control for complaint type. The results point in one direction.

On a 0–1 scale of association strength, complaint type (0.84) and city agency (0.81) dominate. Filing channel is moderate (0.35). Income, month, and population are essentially flat. That already suggests the gap is about what is being reported, not who is reporting it. But high correlation doesn’t prove causation — these variables could be proxies for each other. So we built a sequence of regression models, adding one variable block at a time, and watched two things: how much variance each block explains (R²), and whether the apparent effect of filing channel survives once complaint type is added.

What is nested regression?

Nested regression fits a sequence of models where each step adds a new predictor block on top of the previous one. This shows how much model fit improves as we introduce more information.

Model type used here

We use Ridge linear regression on log(resolution_hours), with one-hot encoded categorical blocks added in sequence.

What is R²?

R² ranges from 0 to 1 and measures the share of outcome variation explained by the model. Higher R² means better in-sample fit.

Drop-one ΔR²

Keep the full model fixed, remove one predictor block, and recompute R². The decrease is that block’s unique contribution beyond the others.

The result is unambiguous. A model using income quartile, month, and filing channel explains only 12.3% of the variance. Adding agency and complaint type pushes R² to 79.2%. More importantly, the phone coefficient — which initially implies complaints are about 6× slower by phone (β = +1.79) — collapses to nearly zero (β = -0.02) once complaint type is added. There is no independent “phones are slow” effect. Phones mostly carry the complaint types that are slow to resolve.

Agency and complaint type are tightly correlated, so we use a full-model drop-one-block test to isolate unique contribution: complaint type contributes ΔR² = 0.126, agency contributes ΔR² = 0.012. Agency still matters, but far less than complaint type once overlap is controlled.

Reading the diagram from the top: economic inequality feeds two parallel mechanisms. The main one is the housing stock. The precise mechanism is not “old buildings,” it is rental housing with deferred maintenance. Manhattan has some of the city’s oldest buildings and also its fastest Q4 resolution times. Age matters only because it correlates with failing boilers and plumbing. The load-bearing cause is landlord disinvestment in aging rental stock: owner-occupiers fix their own boiler, renters file 311.

The second, smaller pathway is digital access. Most of the filing-channel gap is complaint type in disguise, but not all of it. Lower-income residents calling about even the faster complaint types still prefer phones because they have less reliable internet. So filing channel is roughly 90% complaint type in disguise plus a small genuine digital-access effect — real, but dwarfed by the housing pathway.

None of this shows the city’s 311 system treating rich and poor neighborhoods differently. Within matched cells (same agency, same complaint category, same borough, same month) Q4 is faster in only 52.3% of cases — a coin flip. What it shows is structural inequality upstream of 311. The fact that lower-income renters end up in buildings with older boilers, aging plumbing, and recurring pest problems is not a random feature of the world. It is the product of several decades of housing-market dynamics: landlord underinvestment in low-rent units, absentee ownership, rent-regulation incentives that let low-income buildings deteriorate, historical redlining, and concentrated poverty in specific neighborhoods. Those patterns are unequal by any reasonable definition — Q1 renters disproportionately bear the cost of living in a housing stock that has been allowed to decay.

The Explanation

Every 311 complaint falls into one of four categories. Infrastructure covers plumbing, elevators, street conditions, and electrical issues. Health & Safety includes no heat, rodent infestations, unsanitary conditions, and water leaks. Quality of Life means noise, dirty conditions, and graffiti. Other is everything else, such as parking violations, blocked driveways, and abandoned vehicles.

Quality of Life and Other complaints close within a few hours regardless of income. Health & Safety and Infrastructure, however, take far longer. Health & Safety averages 61 to 71 hours across quartiles, while Infrastructure ranges from 66 to 163 hours. Health & Safety shows a modest staircase across quartiles, but the sharpest gradient is in Infrastructure, where wait times rise steadily from 66 hours in Q4 to 163 hours in Q1.

The Infrastructure gradient is driven by what “Infrastructure” actually contains in each income group. Infrastructure complaints fall into two groups. Interior housing repairs include plumbing, elevators, paint/plaster, doors/windows, electrical, and appliances. Public infrastructure covers street conditions, traffic signals, sewers, and water systems. Interior repairs take far longer to resolve, over 200 hours on average across all quartiles. The share of infrastructure complaints that are interior housing repairs rises steadily with poverty. In Q4, it is 28%. In Q3, 34%. In Q2, 47%. And in Q1, 68%. The staircase in resolution time follows this composition shift almost exactly.

Notably, when comparing interior housing repairs alone, Q4 neighborhoods actually take longer to resolve, with a median of 265.6 hours compared to Q1’s 202.1 hours. This confirms that the city is not giving wealthier areas faster service on the same type of problem.

The pattern extends beyond Infrastructure. When we zoom out to all complaint types, the same compositional divide appears across income quartiles. Q1 waits longer because it files far more of the complaints that take longer to fix.

Across all four quartiles, a clear gradient emerges. The poorer the neighborhood, the larger the share of slow-resolving complaints. Health & Safety and Infrastructure account for 37.9% of Q1’s complaints, 28.1% in Q2, 24.5% in Q3, and just 18.6% in Q4. From the wealthiest to the poorest quartile, the complaint mix steadily shifts away from issues that close in hours toward problems that take days or weeks to fix.

An Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition lets us isolate how much of the gap this composition difference actually explains. If Q1 had Q4’s complaint mix but kept its own response speeds, Q1’s expected wait would drop to 1.7 hours, virtually identical to Q4. Complaint mix alone explains 97.8% of the gap.

What is Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition?

A statistical technique originally developed in labor economics to decompose a gap between two groups into two parts: the portion explained by differences in observable characteristics (such as complaint type mix), and a residual that remains after controlling for those differences.

Where does 97.8% come from?

The type-weighted median wait is 12.62 h for Q1 and 1.50 h for Q4, a gap of 11.12 h. If Q1 had Q4’s complaint mix but kept its own response speeds, Q1’s expected wait drops to 1.74 h, leaving only a 0.24 h residual. The explained share is (11.12 − 0.24) / 11.12 = 97.8%.

The composition gap is the mechanism. But it raises a deeper question. Why do Q1 neighborhoods file so many more of these slow-resolving complaints? If the answer is aging housing infrastructure, the data should show a seasonal signature. Heating failures peak in winter. Plumbing and building systems break down more in cold weather. If old buildings are behind the gap, the disparity between quartiles should follow the same rhythm.

The Seasonal Pattern

Across the whole year, a clear pattern emerges in the monthly data. The gap between Q1 and Q4 is largest in winter, reaching over 22 hours in January. It shrinks through spring, bottoms out in June at just 1 hour, then shows a small bump in July and August before dropping again in September. That mid-summer bump comes from infrastructure complaints. Summer heat puts stress on aging plumbing and building systems, and Q1 neighborhoods see the sharpest spike in volume and resolution time. From October onward, the gap climbs steadily back through autumn into another winter peak.

In winter, the four quartiles spread apart dramatically. In January, Q1 waits 25.3 hours while Q4 waits just 3.1. Q2 and Q3 fall in between at around 12–13 hours. By June the lines converge into a narrow band, with all four quartiles between 2.8 and 6.4 hours. The staircase that is so visible in winter nearly flattens in summer.

Q4 almost always resolves fastest throughout the year. Even in June, Q4’s 2.8 hours sits below the other three. This baseline gap exists because the other quartiles, especially Q1, file far more Infrastructure and Health & Safety complaints, such as plumbing failures, elevator outages, rodent infestations, and water leaks. These types take days or weeks to resolve regardless of season, keeping medians higher than Q4’s even in the calmest months.

The baseline inequity is year-round. Winter amplifies it. So what drives the winter spike? One complaint type dominates the answer. HEAT/HOT WATER.

In December, Q1 neighborhoods filed 88.2 HEAT/HOT WATER complaints per 10,000 residents, more than four times Q4’s rate of 21.6. Q2 and Q3 fall in between. In summer, heating complaints drop to near zero across all quartiles and the bars converge. Starting in October, they climb again, with Q1 rising the fastest. This single complaint type accounts for 18.4% of all Q1 complaints in winter but only 1.9% in summer. That massive seasonal volume is what pushes Q1’s overall median dramatically higher in winter, while Q2 and Q3 also rise but less sharply.

The seasonal data confirms what the complaint mix showed. HEAT/HOT WATER, driven by aging boilers in low-income buildings, is the winter amplifier. The year-round baseline comes from Infrastructure and Health & Safety complaints that never go away.

Conclusion

The gap has two layers. The first is a year-round foundation. Lower-income neighborhoods file more Infrastructure and Health & Safety complaints, types that are inherently slow to resolve, creating a baseline inequity that never disappears. The second is a seasonal amplifier. HEAT/HOT WATER floods the caseload in winter, driven by aging boilers and failing heating systems in old buildings. Together, they explain both the persistent gap and its dramatic winter peak.

97.8% of the resolution-time gap is explained by complaint mix alone. Within the same complaint type, wealthier neighborhoods sometimes wait even longer. The patterns in this dataset suggest that differences in complaint mix explain far more of the gap than differences in resolution speed within the same complaint type. The equity gap is, at its root, a housing infrastructure crisis.

The 311 data does not reveal a biased government. It reveals an economic landscape where the burden of failing infrastructure falls disproportionately on those who can least afford better housing. If you are poor, you are more likely to live in an older building that breaks down more often, loses heat in winter, and generates the kinds of complaints that take the longest to resolve. The service gap is real, but it starts with the buildings people live in.
