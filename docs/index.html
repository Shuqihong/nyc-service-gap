NYC 311 Urban Inequality Analysis

Why Do Some New Yorkers
Wait Longer for City Services?

The Hidden Story Behind 3 Million 311 Calls

New York City · Full Year 2024 · 3,187,149 Closed Complaints · 177 Neighborhoods

March 7, 2024. Two New Yorkers wake up to the same emergency: no heat. Both call 311, both get routed to HPD, and both describe the problem in nearly the same words.

On the surface, their situations look identical. But one lives in Cambria Heights, Queens, where the median household income is $147,000. The other lives in Soundview, the South Bronx, where it’s $38,000.

The Queens resident had heat by lunchtime. The Bronx resident waited 17 days.

What is NYC 311?

311 is New York City’s non-emergency government services hotline. Residents call, text, or go online to report problems like broken street lights, noise complaints, lack of heat, or unsanitary conditions. Each request is logged, routed to the responsible agency, and tracked until resolution. The city publishes every request as open data.

These are two real complaints pulled from the city’s own records. Is the city treating rich and poor neighborhoods differently, or is something else going on?

To find out, we analyzed every closed 311 service request filed in New York City in 2024, over 3 million complaints across 177 neighborhoods. The data reveals something more specific than simple discrimination.

The Gap

New York’s 177 neighborhoods, grouped by income into four quartiles, show a clear difference in 311 resolution times. The poorest quarter (Q1) waits a median of 10.8 hours for a 311 complaint to close. The wealthiest quarter (Q4) waits 3.9 hours. Nearly three times as long. Q2 and Q3 fall in between at around 7 hours, forming a staircase from the poorest to the wealthiest neighborhoods.

That pattern is visible at the group level. But when we look across all 3.2 million complaints, the relationship between neighborhood income and resolution time is extremely weak: the correlation is just r = 0.046. In other words, income by itself tells us very little about how long any single complaint will take to resolve. The gap is real, but income alone does not explain it.

What is Pearson correlation (r)?

Pearson correlation r measures linear association between two variables and ranges from −1 to +1. The sign gives direction (positive or negative), and the absolute value gives strength. Values near 0 mean almost no linear relationship.

Statistical Analysis

To look more closely at what is going on, we start with a heat map of the main variables. Two factors stand out immediately. Complaint type (0.84) and city agency (0.81) show the strongest relationships with resolution time. Filing channel is more moderate (0.35), while income, month, and population are all close to flat, which suggests that on their own they say little about how long a complaint will take to close. Complaint type and city agency also have a strong relationship with each other (0.87), and several other variables show intermediate overlaps, so much of what any single variable appears to explain may in fact be shared with the others.

To separate their contributions, we fit a sequence of nested regression models, adding one block of predictors at a time and watching how much each addition actually improves the fit.

What is nested regression?

Nested regression fits a sequence of models where each step adds a new predictor block on top of the previous one. This shows how much model fit improves as we introduce more information.

What is R²?

R² ranges from 0 to 1 and measures the share of outcome variation explained by the model. Multiply by 100 to read it as a percentage (for example, R² = 0.123 means 12.3% explained variation). Higher R² means better in-sample fit.

Drop-one ΔR²

Keep the full model fixed, remove one predictor block, and recompute R². The decrease is that block’s unique contribution beyond the others.

The results are clear. Income quartile, month, and filing channel together explain only 12.3% of the variance in resolution time. Adding city agency and complaint type lifts R² to 79.2%. The filing-channel coefficient tells the same story in miniature. Residents can file a 311 complaint by phone, through the online portal, via the mobile app, or a few smaller routes, and phone is by far the most common channel in lower-income neighborhoods. When we first add filing channel to the model, phone complaints look about 6× slower than online ones (β = +1.79). But once complaint type is controlled for, that coefficient collapses to essentially zero (β = −0.02). There is no independent “phones are slow” effect. Phones simply carry the complaint types that happen to be slow to resolve.

Because agency and complaint type are themselves tightly correlated, a single R² number can hide which of the two is doing the work. We use a drop-one-block test on the full model to isolate what each variable adds on its own. Removing complaint type from the full model drops R² by 12.6 percentage points (ΔR² = 0.126), while removing city agency drops it by only 1.2 percentage points (ΔR² = 0.012). In other words, complaint type carries about ten times as much unique explanatory weight as agency once the overlap between them is accounted for. Put differently, once we know what a complaint is actually about, roughly four fifths of the variation in how long it takes to close is already pinned down. Whatever is left after that is residual noise within complaint types, not a hidden gap between neighborhoods.

The Mechanism

If complaint type carries almost all of the gap, the next question is what those complaints actually are, and why the mix looks so different between the poorest and wealthiest neighborhoods. To answer that, we first need a simpler view of the roughly two hundred raw complaint strings in the data. We group them into four broad categories based on the nature of the issue they describe. Infrastructure covers plumbing, elevators, street conditions, and electrical issues. Health & Safety includes no heat, rodent infestations, unsanitary conditions, and water leaks. Quality of Life means noise, dirty conditions, and graffiti. Other is everything else, such as parking violations, blocked driveways, and abandoned vehicles. Infrastructure covers plumbing, elevators, street conditions, and electrical issues. Health & Safety includes no heat, rodent infestations, unsanitary conditions, and water leaks. Quality of Life means noise, dirty conditions, and graffiti. Other is everything else, such as parking violations, blocked driveways, and abandoned vehicles.

Quality of Life and Other complaints close within a few hours regardless of income. Health & Safety and Infrastructure, however, take far longer. Health & Safety averages 61 to 71 hours across quartiles, while Infrastructure ranges from 66 hours in Q4 to 163 hours in Q1. Infrastructure is also the category with the sharpest gradient, and almost all of that gradient comes from one subgroup. Infrastructure splits naturally into interior housing repairs (plumbing, elevators, paint and plaster, doors and windows, electrical, appliances) and public infrastructure (street conditions, traffic signals, sewers, water systems). Interior repairs take far longer to resolve, over 200 hours on average across all quartiles, and their share of infrastructure complaints climbs steadily with poverty, from 28% in Q4 to 34% in Q3, 47% in Q2, and 68% in Q1. Importantly, when we compare only interior housing repairs to each other, Q4 actually waits a bit longer than Q1 (a median of 265.6 hours versus 202.1 hours). The city is not giving wealthier areas faster service on the same kind of problem. What changes across quartiles is how much of each kind of problem gets filed in the first place.

Splitting Infrastructure this way makes the shift visible at a glance. Interior housing repairs occupy a large slice of Q1’s bar and shrink almost to nothing by Q4, while public infrastructure and Quality of Life grow in the opposite direction. Across all four quartiles, a clear gradient emerges. The poorer the neighborhood, the larger the share of slow-resolving complaints. Health & Safety and Infrastructure together account for 37.9% of Q1’s complaints, 28.1% in Q2, 24.5% in Q3, and just 18.6% in Q4.

The regression told us how much of the variance in resolution time is accounted for by complaint type. It did not tell us how much of the eleven-hour gap between Q1 and Q4 would disappear if the two quartiles filed the same mix of complaints. For that we need a different kind of question, a counterfactual one. An Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition answers it directly. It takes Q4’s complaint mix, applies it to Q1 while holding Q1’s own response speeds fixed, and reports what Q1’s median wait would have been under that hypothetical.

What is Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition?

A statistical technique originally developed in labor economics to decompose a gap between two groups into two parts: the portion explained by differences in observable characteristics (such as complaint type mix), and a residual that remains after controlling for those differences.

Where does 97.8% come from?

The type-weighted median wait is 12.62 h for Q1 and 1.50 h for Q4, a gap of 11.12 h. If Q1 had Q4’s complaint mix but kept its own response speeds, Q1’s expected wait drops to 1.74 h, leaving only a 0.24 h residual. The explained share is (11.12 − 0.24) / 11.12 = 97.8%.

The result is striking. Q1’s expected wait under Q4’s mix drops to 1.7 hours, almost identical to Q4’s actual 1.5 hours. Out of the 11.1-hour gap, only 0.24 hours survive once the composition difference is removed. Complaint mix alone explains 97.8% of the gap between Q1 and Q4. The remaining 2.2% is the most any within-type speed difference could possibly account for.

The composition gap is the mechanism. But it raises a deeper question. Why do Q1 neighborhoods file so many more of these slow-resolving complaints? If the answer is aging housing infrastructure, the data should show a seasonal signature. Heating failures peak in winter. Plumbing and building systems break down more in cold weather. If old buildings are behind the gap, the disparity between quartiles should follow the same rhythm.

Baseline and Winter Amplifier

The gap we have been studying is not a single number. It has two layers stacked on top of each other. A year-round baseline that never disappears, and a winter amplifier that briefly makes the gap three or four times larger. Separating these two layers is what this section is about.

Before going further, it is worth noting a subtlety that might look like a contradiction. In the earlier heat map and regression, the variable month on its own barely moved resolution time at all. Yet here we are about to show a very large seasonal effect. Both are true. The seasonal story is not a main effect of month. It is a three-way interaction between month, income quartile, and complaint type. Pooled across all complaints and all neighborhoods, month washes out. Once we split by quartile and watch how the complaint mix itself shifts with the calendar, a clear pattern appears.

The year-round baseline

Q4 is the fastest quartile in almost every month of the year. Even in June, when all four quartiles converge into a narrow band between 2.8 and 6.4 hours, Q4’s 2.8 hours still sits below the other three. This baseline gap exists because Q1, Q2, and Q3 file far more Infrastructure and Health & Safety complaints, such as plumbing failures, elevator outages, rodent infestations, and water leaks. Those types take days or weeks to resolve regardless of season, so they keep Q1 through Q3 medians higher than Q4’s even in the calmest months. The baseline is inequitable year-round.

The winter amplifier

On top of that baseline, winter adds a dramatic spike. In January, Q1 waits 25.3 hours while Q4 still waits only 3.1. Q2 and Q3 fall in between at around 12 to 13 hours. The gap between Q1 and Q4 reaches over 22 hours in January, shrinks through spring, bottoms out in June at just 1 hour, bumps up slightly in July and August as summer heat stresses aging plumbing, and then climbs back through autumn into another winter peak. The staircase that is so visible in winter nearly flattens in summer.

So what drives the winter spike? One complaint type dominates the answer. HEAT/HOT WATER.

In December, Q1 neighborhoods filed 88.2 HEAT/HOT WATER complaints per 10,000 residents, more than four times Q4’s rate of 21.6, with Q2 and Q3 in between. In summer, heating complaints drop to near zero across all quartiles and the bars converge. Starting in October they climb again, with Q1 rising the fastest. This single complaint type accounts for 18.4% of all Q1 complaints in winter but only 1.9% in summer.

This is the three-way interaction made concrete. Month alone does not slow the city down, and income alone does not slow it down, and a complaint type alone does not slow it down. What slows Q1 down is the particular combination: in winter, Q1 files a surge of a complaint type that is inherently slow to resolve. The calendar shifts the complaint mix, and the mix shift is concentrated in the poorest quartile. Neither month nor income would look like much of a driver on its own, but together they reshape Q1’s complaint distribution and drag its median upward. The baseline inequity is year-round; the winter amplifier is a seasonal rearrangement of the mix.

The Full Picture

Everything so far has traced the proximate cause of the gap. Lower-income neighborhoods wait longer because they file more of the complaint types that are slow to resolve, and because in winter those slow types surge. But this only pushes the question one step back. Why do poorer neighborhoods file so many more heat failures, plumbing breakdowns, and pest problems in the first place? The 311 data itself does not answer this. The answer lies outside the dataset, in the structure of the housing market and the distribution of digital infrastructure. The root cause is economic inequality, and it reaches the 311 system through two parallel pathways, one dominant and one secondary.

The dominant pathway runs through housing. In a market where rent takes up a rising share of low-income household budgets, the buildings that the poorest residents can afford are disproportionately older, smaller, and under the control of landlords whose financial incentives do not favor proactive maintenance. Matthew Desmond’s fieldwork in Evicted (2016) documents this dynamic in detail. In low-rent buildings, owners calculate that the cost of replacing a failing boiler or repairing chronic plumbing leaks often exceeds the marginal rent they can extract from the unit, so they defer the work, sometimes indefinitely, while tenants with little bargaining power absorb the consequences. Tenants who own their homes can fix a leak themselves the same day. Tenants who rent can only file a complaint and wait. The renter-owner split is therefore not a demographic detail, it is the switch that converts a private maintenance problem into a public 311 ticket. NYU’s Furman Center, in its annual State of New York City’s Housing and Neighborhoods reports, has shown repeatedly that serious housing deficiencies, including rodent infestations, water leaks, and heating breakdowns, are concentrated in lower-income ZIP codes. HUD’s American Housing Survey finds similar patterns nationally. None of this is a coincidence of geography. It is the legacy of decades of housing policy choices, from the mid-century redlining maps documented by Richard Rothstein in The Color of Law (2017) to contemporary patterns of landlord disinvestment in rent-stabilized low-rent buildings. When we see HEAT/HOT WATER complaints flood Q1 neighborhoods in January, we are watching that accumulated history finally break through the surface of the municipal data.

The secondary pathway runs through digital access. The Pew Research Center’s ongoing broadband and smartphone surveys have documented a persistent gap in reliable home internet, computer ownership, and day-to-day comfort with digital tools between lower- and higher-income Americans. In New York specifically, Census and city broadband reports show the same gap at the neighborhood level. A resident with fast home internet and an up-to-date phone will usually file a non-urgent 311 request through the NYC311 website or mobile app. A resident without those things, or who has to share a device, will usually pick up the phone. This does not, as our regression showed, make the city answer phone complaints more slowly. But it does mean that filing channel carries a small independent signature of the digital divide, layered on top of the much larger housing pathway.

The diagram below makes the full chain visible. Economic inequality sits at the top. It feeds the two upstream mechanisms, which in turn reshape what neighborhoods file and how they file it. The thick red arrow in the middle of the diagram marks the direct causal edge that does almost all of the work: the complaint mix itself drives resolution time, because some complaint types are inherently slow to fix and others are not.

A few details in the diagram are worth reading carefully. First, the arrow from complaint mix to resolution time is drawn bold and red because the Oaxaca decomposition showed that this single edge accounts for 97.8% of the gap between the poorest and wealthiest quartiles. Everything else in the diagram feeds into it or rides on top of it. Second, the winter amplifier is drawn as a branch off the complaint-mix node, not as an independent cause. Winter does not slow the city down on its own. It rearranges the seasonal mix of complaint types in lower-income neighborhoods, and that rearrangement is what produces the winter spike. Third, the arrow from filing channel is drawn dashed and marked “≈0 after controls,” to make it clear that the phone signal disappears once complaint type is held fixed. It is a real marker of the digital divide, but it is not an independent slowdown mechanism. Finally, the outcome node is labeled simply “longer 311 resolution time,” not “longer wait in Q1.” The mechanism is general. It produces slower waits in any neighborhood whose complaint mix tilts toward slow housing-interior issues, and faster waits in any neighborhood whose mix tilts toward fast, externally visible issues. Income is how neighborhoods end up on one side of that tilt or the other, but the mechanism itself is about complaint composition, not zip-code labels.

None of this shows the city’s 311 system treating rich and poor neighborhoods differently. Within matched cells (same agency, same complaint category, same borough, same month) the wealthier quartile is faster in only 52.3% of cases, essentially a coin flip. What the diagram shows is structural inequality upstream of 311. The 311 dataset is not the site of the inequity. It is the mirror that makes the inequity legible.

Conclusion

The gap has two layers. The first is a year-round foundation. Lower-income neighborhoods file more Infrastructure and Health & Safety complaints, types that are inherently slow to resolve, creating a baseline inequity that never disappears. The second is a seasonal amplifier. HEAT/HOT WATER floods the caseload in winter, driven by aging boilers and failing heating systems in old buildings. Together, they explain both the persistent gap and its dramatic winter peak.

97.8% of the resolution-time gap is explained by complaint mix alone. Within the same complaint type, wealthier neighborhoods sometimes wait even longer. The patterns in this dataset suggest that differences in complaint mix explain far more of the gap than differences in resolution speed within the same complaint type. The equity gap is, at its root, a housing infrastructure crisis.

The 311 data does not reveal a biased government. It reveals an economic landscape where the burden of failing infrastructure falls disproportionately on those who can least afford better housing. If you are poor, you are more likely to live in an older building that breaks down more often, loses heat in winter, and generates the kinds of complaints that take the longest to resolve. The service gap is real, but it starts with the buildings people live in.
